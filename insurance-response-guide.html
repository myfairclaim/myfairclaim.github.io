What to Expect After Submission

Understanding the claim process and typical insurance company responses

‚úì You've Submitted Your Claim. Now you'll navigate the insurance response process. This guide helps you identify their tactics and respond professionally.
üìÖ Days 1-5: Initial Review

What's Happening: The insurance company receives your claim and assigns it to an adjuster. They'll review your documentation.

What to Expect:

  • You may receive an acknowledgment email or letter
  • They might request additional documentation (delay tactic)
  • An adjuster may call to "discuss" the claim

Your Action: Wait patiently. Do NOT provide recorded statements without reviewing the Phone Scripts tab first.

üìÖ Days 6-15: Assessment Period

What's Happening: The adjuster is evaluating your claim and determining their strategy.

What to Expect:

  • You might receive a lowball settlement offer
  • They may claim they need "more time to investigate"
  • You could get a request for a recorded statement
  • They might dispute your valuation methodology

Your Action: Use the Response Templates tab to determine what strategy they're using and select the appropriate response.

üìÖ Days 16-30: Decision Window

What's Happening: Most states require insurers to accept or deny claims within 30 days.

What to Expect:

  • You'll receive either: (1) Acceptance & payment, (2) Denial letter, or (3) Continued delay tactics
  • If denied, they'll provide a reason (often disputing valuation or claiming policy exclusions)
  • If delayed, they're hoping you'll give up

Your Action: If denied or delayed past 30 days, escalate using the Response Templates or proceed to the Dispute Resolution Guide.

Click the tactic you're experiencing to access professional response templates

1
Initial Lowball Offer
They offer an amount significantly lower than your documented diminished value, hoping you'll accept less.
2
Recorded Statement Request
They ask for a recorded statement, hoping to get you to say something that undermines your claim.
3
Delay Tactics
Repeated requests for "additional documentation" or claims of "ongoing investigation" to stall the process.
4
Comparative Negligence
They claim you were partially at fault for the accident to reduce or deny your diminished value claim.
5
"Market Value" Disputes
They challenge your professional valuation with their own "internal assessment" showing lower diminished value.
6
Excessive Documentation
They request unnecessary documentation hoping you'll give up or make an error in providing information.
